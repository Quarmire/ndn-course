What you leave with

Contributor status, not a certificate
The final capstone is a PR-quality contribution to ndn-workspace or NDF, taken through design review, implementation, witness tests, and review rounds — the same flow the maintainers use. Capstone B · a mergeable-quality contribution with your name on it
Fluency on large codebases
You train inside a ~60-crate, multi-repo workspace from day one: navigating, reading tests as documentation, and growing it under its own dependency rules. That skill transfers to any big Rust project. M1 scavenger hunt → M12 your own crate, placed under the workspace's growth doctrine
The machine, not just the syntax
Every module has an under-the-hood strand — assembly on Compiler Explorer, measured allocation costs, cache-line labs, what .await compiles to — so you derive answers instead of memorizing them. benchmarks and asm readings attached to your own code, every phase
Debugging as a discipline
Bugs are a when, not an if. You learn both halves of the covenant: prevent what you can (types, assertions, clippy, tests) and diagnose the rest fast (debuggers, bisect, hypothesis logs) — in code written to be diagnosable, with honest comments and logs that earn their place. M5 planted-bug hunts · a bug journal reviewed at every checkpoint
Architectural judgment
You practice the genre of real design decisions — written decision notes in the project's own ratified format, defended under review — before both capstones require a plan you then execute methodically. M14 decision note at ratifiable quality · proposal-gated capstones

How it works

toy → real → constraint

Every concept lands three times: you build a toy version, you find the production version in the workspace, then you use it under constraint — refactor katas, signature-locked drills, planted-bug hunts.

Recognition isn't recall, and recall isn't fluency. The loop is how a concept becomes a muscle.

Three gates, one tutor

Every submission passes a mechanical gate (tests, clippy, fmt), a rubric review covering design, comments, and log quality, and a reflection where you defend your choices.

An LLM tutor rides along the whole way — Socratic by policy: hints escalate, answers never arrive before your first attempt.

Curated, not conjured

Readings are drawn from the codebase itself, its design vault, and the best writing in the field — from CPU internals to atomics to debugging classics — a web of real knowledge, not just one book.

The Rust Book stays linked as a reference. It is never assigned as homework.

Built on a living codebase — on purpose

A course frozen against finished software teaches archaeology. This one is pinned to tagged snapshots of an actively developed stack, so your learning path is stable while your capstone points at the moving frontier — and your contribution lands somewhere that matters.

  • pinned · every course release locks to named repo tags
  • tested · the 3-command quick start runs in CI on a fresh container
  • honest · the project documents its own known failures; so will you
  • graded by oracles · conformance vectors, not vibes
